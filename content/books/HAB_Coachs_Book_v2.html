Preface

How to Use This Book

A great curriculum coached badly produces nothing. A modest curriculum coached well produces champions.

Your Position in the System

You are the single most important person in the HAB Sales OS. The curriculum can be the greatest in the world — if you don't run the cadence, score the ride-alongs, deliver SBI feedback, and protect the system from drift, it dies in a drawer. Your role is to convert a binder into behavior change, week after week, advisor after advisor. The Advisor Book tells your team WHAT to do. This book tells you HOW to install it, measure it, and keep it alive — without losing the advisor.

Coaching is the systematic reduction of the advisor's uncertainty — the same principle the advisor applies to the customer, applied one level up.

Your Reading Plan

  • Week 0: Read Chapters 1–4 cover-to-cover. Sign the Coach's Oath.
  • Week 1: Begin Phase 1 of the 90-Day Plan (Chapter 14). Start running the rhythm in Chapter 5 immediately.
  • Ongoing: Reference Chapter 6 whenever a KPI goes red, Chapter 7 before every ride-along, Chapter 11 when someone stalls. Re-read Chapters 3–4 quarterly to keep your own coaching sharp.
  • Months 4–6: Run the 13-Week Coach Certification Cycle (Chapter 15) to build the shop's own coach so the system survives you.

What You Should Have Within Arm's Reach

  • This book — for coaching depth.
  • The Advisor Book v2.0 — so you can reference exactly what the advisors are learning, script by script.
  • The Pocket Card — on your boom board.
  • The Shop KPI Scoreboard — wall-mounted, color-coded, updated every Friday.

The advisor controls behavior. You control whether they keep getting better at it.

What a Coach Actually Does

A coach in this system is not a manager who happens to give feedback. A coach is a designer of behavior change. Three things, every week, without fail:

  1. Observes — rides along, audits calls, scores against the scorecards.
  2. Reflects — delivers SBI feedback (Situation · Behavior · Impact), one written note per advisor per workday.
  3. Compounds — holds the weekly cadence so improvement stacks week-over-week instead of resetting.

The Six Coaching Principles

PrincipleWhat It Means in Practice
1 · Specific Beats Sincere"You did a great job" is worthless. "On the Subaru call, you handed the photo across the counter while saying the Risk line — that's exactly the Media Confirmation move" is gold. Always name the observable behavior.
2 · One Thing At a TimeEnd every session with ONE behavior change. Three is zero. An advisor can consciously work on one new behavior per week — pick the highest-leverage one and park the rest.
3 · Behavior, Not Identity"You skipped Risk on three of five PRS items today," not "you're not a closer." Identity-level criticism creates defensiveness; behavior-level criticism creates change.
4 · Praise In Public, Correct In PrivatePublic correction is humiliation, even when the words are kind. Public praise multiplies; public correction shuts the team down for a week. This is math, not soft policy.
5 · Coach the Process, Score the OutcomeReward running the bases in order even when the customer doesn't authorize. The advisor controls behavior, not whether a specific customer says yes. Coach the inputs; score the outputs. Never reverse this.
6 · Coach the Rare Skill, Not the Easy OneIf an advisor is already a 90% Rapport closer, don't keep drilling Rapport. Find the skill stuck at 30% — the listening, the silence, the no-kitchen-sink discipline. The rare skill is where the next level lives.

Where Coaching Fails (and How to Avoid It)

Failure PatternWhat's HappeningFix
"I'm too busy to coach this week."Operations ate the cadence. Install slows immediately.Owner blocks the coach's morning calendar. Non-negotiable. Operational fires get covered by SAs.
"Great call, keep it up."Vague feedback rewires nothing.Every note in SBI format. If you can't name the Situation and the Behavior, you weren't observing.
Correcting three things in one 1:1.The advisor leaves with zero.One gap per session — the most coachable one.
Coaching the number instead of the step."Get your authorization rate up" is a wish, not a coaching move.Use the red-flag map (Chapter 6): trace the number to the upstream step and drill that.
Promoting on a feeling.Promotions become political; trust in the ladder erodes.Evidence bars in Chapter 10. Show it, don't claim it.
🔷 Coach's Corner · The mirror Everything you're about to require of advisors — scripts, drills, mastery checks, self-audits — this book requires of you. Your drills are ride-alongs. Your script is SBI. Your mastery check is the Coach Certification in Chapter 15. Your scoreboard is the advisor team's quarterly level change. Model it.

Chapter 2

The System You're Coaching — Compressed Reference

You cannot coach what you cannot see. This chapter is the whole Advisor system on a few pages — the full depth lives in the Advisor Book v2.0.

The Core Principle

Sales is not persuasion. Sales is the systematic reduction of uncertainty. We do not sell repairs. We manufacture certainty — and certainty buys repairs by itself.

The customer enters uncertain, fearful, or defensive; they leave educated, confident, and loyal. Every step removes one specific uncertainty. When a step fails, the advisor didn't "lose the sale" — they left an uncertainty in place. Your diagnosis always starts with: which uncertainty survived?

The 8 Steps and What Each Removes

#StepUncertainty RemovedCoach Listens For
1Establish Rapport"Am I going to be sold or helped here?"Smile in the voice, the pause, the relief line, name ×3, no price quoted.
2Establish Credibility"Are these people competent?"Full 90-second tour, warranty unprompted, transport offered, no bragging.
3Collect Data / Custody"Do they understand my situation?"Symptom translated to concern, boom board complete, East-West used, customer separated from vehicle.
4Inspect & Analyze"Are they making this up?"Full DVI, media on every Yellow/Red, walk-the-bay within 15 min, Green/Yellow/Red by the spec question.
5Build & Strategize"What should I do, in what order?"Diamond order locked before the call, PRS built for every Red, Good-Good-Bad planned, no kitchen-sink.
6Present"Why this, why now, what's it cost?"10-step ISO, media confirmation, re-engagement every 60–90s, NO pause between Investment and Coverage.
7Overcome ObjectionsThe customer's remaining doubtsListen first, never argue, never "but," the right Boss script, re-ask for the close.
8Execute the CloseThe decision momentAssumptive / choice / direction-facing close; Home Plate run warm and complete.

Module 9 — L.A.S.T. Recovery: Listen → Acknowledge → Solve → Thank, in order, every time. Banned word: "policy." 24-hour status-update window. The Service Recovery Paradox: a well-recovered complaint customer becomes more loyal than one who never had a problem.

The Trust Triangle

Every customer silently grades the advisor on Competence ("do they know what they're doing?"), Transparency ("are they hiding anything?"), and Trustworthiness ("would they do this for family?"). Score all three → price becomes irrelevant. Lose any one → price becomes the only conversation. When you hear a price objection, diagnose which leg broke — it's usually not price.

The Baseball Diamond

BasePlayContent
1st — ISOLATESell the customer's original concern first.The thing they came in for (or safety if no concern). Without 1st base, no customer cares what else we found.
2nd — EXPANDAdditional findings, separate call.Red items from the DVI, in PRS order, most safety-critical first.
Short Stop — SAVEMissed-sale re-open, fresh voice.The "let me think about it" turn. Missed-Sale Recovered % lives here.
3rd — PROTECTMaintenance, soft sell.Yellow items: fluids by interval, alignment, etc. Real opt-out offered.
Home — REINFORCEFinal review + next visit.Confirm the work, benefits, direction-facing close. Retention is built here.

Hang up between bases — encouraged. Slower per call, materially higher close rate. Kitchen-sinking is the #1 trust killer.

The Vehicle Health Dashboard

One question per DVI finding: "Does it operate within manufacturer specifications?" GREEN — yes, in spec: notify healthy (primes Good-Good-Bad). YELLOW — yes, but flag: watch or schedule maintenance; an offer, not an ask. RED — no, out of spec: full PRS, strategize the base, must address. The rule is binary on the spec question, not on perceived severity — coach advisors who soften a Red into a Yellow because they don't want to push. A broken stereo button and a broken brake pad are the same rule; where they get sold is strategy (Chapter 5 of the Advisor Book).

The 10-Step ISO Presentation

1 Greeting + frame · 2 Recap concern (co-review) · 3 Media confirmation · 4 Health overview · 5 Walk the green · 6 Walk the yellow · 7 Walk the red (PRS each) · 8 Investment · 9 Coverage · 10 Direction question. The structural rule: 8 and 9 are one beat, not two. The pause between Investment and Coverage is where deals die — score it as a critical gap on every ride-along.

The 8 Boss Battles (catalog + coaching lever)

BossObjectionThe Lever You're Coaching Toward
1"I'm selling the car"Reframe against the real alternative — cost-of-replacement math, not repair vs. $0.
2"Price is too high"Anti-Lowest-Price Brag. Never apologize for price, never disparage the competitor — raise your own bar.
3"No money right now"Money Reframe — "no money" means "not today"; pivot to financing, stay on the line for the approval.
4"Talk to my spouse"Three-Way Bypass — offer to deliver the PRS to the spouse directly so it isn't retold second-hand.
5"I'll wait" / "just the safety stuff"Clarify safety vs. maintenance. Agree, defer, and schedule the deferred items before they leave.
6"You're always selling"Pro Obligation Frame — from salesperson to fiduciary; the customer keeps control.
7"I need my car today"Solve logistics first (loaner/Uber/pickup), then return to the repair.
8"Parts & labor breakdown?"Refuse the apples-to-apples frame — protection program, not parts and minutes.

Universal rules: never argue · acknowledge first · find the real concern · pivot to value or risk, never discount · re-ask for the close. Full battle scripts: Advisor Book Chapter 7.

The Mastery Ladder

1 Rookie (reads scripts) → 2 Advisor (runs scripts naturally, concern vs. symptom) → 3 Closer (runs the bases, PRS by heart, East-West) → 4 Pro (defeats all 8 Bosses, re-opens missed sales) → 5 Champion (requested by name, trains others, owns culture). Advisor → Closer is the hardest jump; Pro → Champion is the longest. Promotion bars: Chapter 10.

Chapter 3

SBI Feedback — The Only Format We Use

Every coaching note, every 1:1, every ride-along debrief uses the same three-part sentence. Praise and correction alike.

The SBI Sentence

"On [Situation], you [Behavior], which [Impact]."

The sentence has three slots, in order. Skip any slot and the feedback collapses:

PartWhat Goes In the SlotExample
1 · SituationWhen and where — specific, recent, observed."On the 11am call with Mrs. Garcia…"
2 · BehaviorWhat they did or didn't do — observable, not interpreted."…you stated the price and then paused…"
3 · ImpactWhy it mattered — the consequence in the room."…and she opened the price negotiation."

Worked SBI Sentences

🟨 Worked Examples · Correction and praise

Correction: "On the Civic ABS call this morning, you paused after stating the investment, which gave Maria room to bring up the price at her brother's shop."

Correction: "On the F-150 cooling system PRS, you led with the photo of the leak instead of leading with the risk, which is why she said 'so it just looks bad?' instead of authorizing."

Praise: "On the Tahoe walk-in at 8am, you offered the tour without being asked, which is why the DVI conversation didn't feel like a hard sell when we got there."

Praise: "On the 11am call, you said the investment number and went immediately into the warranty without pausing. The customer didn't flinch and authorized in the next breath — that's the Level-3 close I've been looking for."

Vague vs. SBI

TypeVague (don't do this)SBI (do this)
Praise"Great call!""On the 11am call (S), you went from investment straight into warranty with no pause (B). The customer authorized in the next breath (I)."
Correction"You need to do better on objections.""On the 2pm call with the spouse objection (S), you went straight to discounting instead of offering the three-way call (B). That sets a precedent that complaining gets discounts, and it killed our chance to talk to the actual decision-maker (I). Let's drill Boss 4 together this afternoon."
🟢 Drill · The SBI Rewrite Setup: collect five pieces of feedback you gave (or heard) this week. Rewrite each as a full three-slot SBI sentence, out loud.
Score: all three slots present? Behavior observable? Impact concrete?
Goal: one written SBI note per advisor per workday, without exception. An audit that doesn't produce an SBI note didn't happen.
⚪ Pro Tip · SBI works in both directions Most coaches use structure only for correction and go vague on praise. Specific praise rewires faster than generic praise — and it's what earns you the right to be specific when you correct.

Chapter 4

The Five-Beat Coaching Conversation

For 1:1s and any hard talk. Run it in order — the structure is what keeps the relationship intact.

The Five Beats

BeatWhatSample WordsTime
1 · Open with the winSpecific, recent, observed. Sets the conversation as collaborative, not punitive. (Two minutes of human first — no agenda.)"How's your week going?" then "On Tuesday's call you…"2–4 min
2 · Name the gap (SBI)One gap. The most coachable one. Specific Situation → Behavior → Impact."On the F-150 PRS, you skipped the Risk, which is why she deferred."2 min
3 · Ask, don't tellThe advisor's answer reveals whether this is a knowledge gap, a courage gap, or a system gap. Coach the actual cause."What was happening when you skipped the Risk?" · "Walk me through how that call landed for you."4 min
4 · Co-design the practiceThe advisor proposes, the coach refines. Self-selected drills get done; assigned drills get resented."What drill would help? When are you going to do it?"2 min
5 · Confirm the commitmentOne sentence the advisor writes down. That sentence becomes Friday's check-in. Close with belief."Tomorrow I will lead with Risk on every red PRS item before showing the photo." · "You're one cycle away from Pro."2 min

Twelve minutes total. It scales from a 5-minute hot debrief to a 30-minute quarterly check-in. The order matters — especially beats 1 and 5.

⚪ Pro Tip · Get the advisor's read before you give yours In beat 3, ALWAYS ask what they thought of the call before offering your view. They usually already know what went wrong (so you're reinforcing self-coaching, not lecturing), and they hear correction from themselves far better than from you. Coaching is mostly Socratic.
🔴 Stop · One ask per session End every session with ONE behavior change. Three is zero. If you're tempted to add a second ask, write it down for next week instead.
🔷 Coach's Corner · When the conversation needs to be hard Run the same five beats even when it feels mechanical — the structure protects both people. The dedicated hard-talk escalation (repeated non-improvement) has its own protocol in Chapter 11. Never run either conversation in the bullpen.

For each of the 8 steps, score 0–3:

ScoreMeaning
0Skipped.
1Attempted, missing key elements.
2Ran the step, minor gaps.

Chapter 8

Call Audits & Drill Design

One audited call per advisor per day, one written SBI note each — and drills built from what the audits reveal.

The Daily Audit Protocol

  1. Pull at least one call (or workflow) per advisor per day. Rotate call types: inbound, presentation, recovery.
  2. Score against the current focus metric for the week — not against everything at once.
  3. Write one SBI note per advisor. An audit that doesn't produce an SBI note didn't happen.
  4. Deliver praise notes same-day and publicly; deliver correction notes privately, ideally inside a Five-Beat micro-conversation.
⚪ Pro Tip · Read the RO like a cold tech For custody audits, read the documented concern as if you were the tech walking into the bay cold. If you can't reproduce the customer's symptom from the writing alone, the intake broke down — rehearse it aloud with the advisor. Don't scold. Rehearse.

Drill Design — The Rules

A good drill isolates ONE behavior, takes five minutes, and repeats daily until reflexive.

RuleWhy
One behavior per drill.A drill that practices three things practices none. "The pause" is a drill. "Better phone calls" is a wish.
Five minutes, standing, out loud.Reps build instinct; instinct survives pressure. Sitting discussion builds opinions.
Scored, 1–3 or 1–10.What gets scored gets repeated. Post the leaderboard for team drills.
Repeats daily until reflexive.Then retire it and rotate the next behavior in. Don't hold a drill past mastery — boredom kills the huddle.
Sourced from this week's audits.Listen for the one behavior that keeps repeating across advisors — that's tomorrow's drill.
🟢 Drill · Design One Now Setup: take this week's audit notes. Find the behavior that appeared most across advisors.
Rep: design a five-minute drill for that one behavior — setup, rep, score, goal — and run it at tomorrow's Take-Five.
Goal: a full week of Take-Fives running on drills you designed from your own audit notes. (This is Coach Certification Week 7.)

Chapter 9

Roleplay Mastery — Playing the Customer

The coach must be the shop's toughest customer. The drill is only as good as the customer you play.

How to Hold an Objection

  1. Pick a persona — vehicle, concern, worry, budget. The richer the persona, the better the rep. (Personas: friendly first-timer, comparison shopper with another quote, hostile repeat customer, analytical spouse on a three-way, hot counter complainant.)
  2. Hold the objection realistically. Don't fold on the first counter — a real customer doesn't. Give ground only when the advisor runs the actual script move.
  3. Escalate if the advisor argues. Arguing is the failure mode; make it cost something in the roleplay, exactly like it costs on the floor.
  4. Score the response against the script, not against your mood. The script book is the rubric — acknowledge first? real concern found? value pivot, no discount? re-ask for the close?

The Boss Rush Protocol

🟢 Drill · The Boss Rush (team, 15 minutes, daily in Phase 2) Setup: all hands, standing. Coach throws random Boss lines from the eight; advisors counter in turn, no notes.
Score: 1–3 per counter. Leaderboard posted. Track who has cleared all eight in live calls within 30 days.
Escalation: once the team clears the basic lines, add persona pressure — interruptions, emotion, the second objection stacked behind the first.
Goal: the fastest path to Level 4 (Pro) on the team.
⚪ Pro Tip · Pick tomorrow's boss from today's calls Listen for the objection your advisors handle worst this week — that's tomorrow's boss. Drill the weakness, not the favorite.
🔷 Coach's Corner · Scoring a roleplay rep For each counter, mark four checkpoints: (1) acknowledged first, no "but"; (2) found the real concern with a clarifying question; (3) pivoted to value or risk, never discount; (4) re-asked for the close. 4/4 = 3 points. 3/4 = 2. Anything less = 1, and the advisor re-runs it immediately — the re-run is where the learning happens.

Chapter 11

The Stalled Advisor — Diagnose Before You Prescribe

Stuck at the same KPI for 3+ weeks? The cause is one of three gaps. Prescribe drills for a system problem and you lose the advisor to frustration.

The Three Gaps

GapWhat It Looks LikeThe Solution
1 · Knowledge gapThey don't know the script or technique. Asked to run it, they can't.Re-teach the specific module. Drill it daily for 5 days. Mastery check.
2 · Courage gapThey know the script but won't use it — afraid of the customer's reaction.Do 3 ride-alongs in a row where the coach demonstrates the move live. Courage is borrowed before it's earned.
3 · System gapSomething in the shop prevents the skill: no DVI tablet, no quiet space for calls, scheduling chaos.Fix the system. Coaching cannot overcome a broken system.

The diagnostic question is Five-Beat beat 3: "What was happening when you skipped the Risk?" The advisor's answer tells you which gap you're looking at. Coach the actual cause.

The Difficult Conversation — The Advisor Who Won't Improve

Every coach will face the advisor who attends drills, nods along, and changes nothing. This is not a coaching problem — it's a clarity problem. Run this conversation, in this order, exactly once:

  1. Name the pattern, not the person. "Over the last three weeks, I've scored PRS Completeness at 60%, 55%, 50%. That's a trend, and it's gone the wrong direction."
  2. Ask what's in the way. "What's making this hard?" Then actually listen for 30 seconds without interrupting. Sometimes it's a personal issue, a tool problem, or a misunderstanding of the standard.
  3. Make the standard explicit and observable. "The standard for this role is 100% PRS on every finding. Here's what that looks like in practice."
  4. Set a clear 30-day improvement gate. "In 30 days, the metric needs to be Green for two consecutive weeks. Here's the support I'll provide: [specific drills, ride-alongs, 1:1 cadence]. Here's what happens if it doesn't: [next step]."
  5. Document, then follow through. Either they improve — praise the climb out — or the conversation you've already had becomes the foundation for the next step. Coaching that has no consequence isn't coaching — it's wishing.
🔴 Stop · Never in the bullpen Office with the door closed, or off-site if necessary. A public version of this conversation is humiliating for the advisor, terrifying for the team, and destroys psychological safety across the whole shop.

When to Stop Coaching and Escalate

Some situations don't belong in coaching — they belong with the owner. Surface each with specific SBI documentation. Do not let coaching become a way to avoid a hard staffing decision.

  • Advisor is consistently late or absent — attendance is not a coaching problem.
  • Advisor refuses to use the script in roleplay — not a knowledge or courage gap; a willingness gap.
  • Advisor is acting against the HAB Oath knowingly (e.g., quoting on the phone after being corrected three times).
  • Advisor is creating friction in the shop culture.
  • Three full 90-day cycles without progression past Rookie.

Chapter 12

Coaching Recovery — Auditing L.A.S.T.

A complaint is a coaching rep. Audit every recovery, protect the banned-word rule, and convert recovered customers into reviews.

What You're Auditing

Score every recovery against the four beats, in order:

BeatPass Looks LikeFail Looks Like
L — Listen30+ seconds uninterrupted. "Okay." "I understand." "Go ahead."Interrupting to defend or explain.
A — AcknowledgeEmotion mirrored before facts. "And," never "but.""I understand, BUT…" — the "but" deletes everything before it.
S — SolveSpecific, time-bound, owned: "I will call you personally by 3pm.""We'll get back to you." Vague ages into a Yelp review.
T — ThankComplaint reframed as a gift; loyalty locked in.Skipped because "they're still upset."

The Coach's Recovery Protocol

  1. 48-hour audit. Any time an advisor handles a complaint, walk through the interaction with them within 48 hours. Not punitive — coaching, using the Advisor Book's Recovery Mastery Check as the scorecard.
  2. 24-hour window enforcement. Any unresolved complaint must have a status update to the customer within 24 hours, even if the resolution isn't ready. Silence past 24 hours doubles the loyalty damage.
  3. The follow-up call. You (or the manager) personally call the recovered customer 5–7 days after resolution. Two purposes: confirm satisfaction, and ask for a review. Well-recovered customers leave the best reviews the shop will ever get.
  4. Praise the Listen, not the Solve. Most coaches default to praising the fix. The Listen is the skill 95% of advisors get wrong — praising the rare skill rewires the team faster.
🔴 Stop · Listen for the word "policy" anywhere in the shop "Policy" is banned in recovery — it tells the customer the rule matters more than they do. When you hear it, replace it on the spot with what we'll actually do: "the way we handle this is…" Also banned as first moves: defending, explaining, and discounting before the problem is solved.
🔷 Coach's Corner · The repeat-complaint red flag If the same advisor produces three or more complaints in 30 days tracing to the same root — communication, follow-through, attention to detail — it's a behavior pattern, not bad luck. Address the underlying skill directly, not just the L.A.S.T. execution. And check the recovery log first whenever the 5-Star Review KPI drops: unrecovered complaints kill review averages fastest.
🟢 Drill · Score a Real Recovery Setup: pull one real complaint from this week.
Rep: score it against all four L.A.S.T. beats (1–5 each) with the advisor present, using the after-action worksheet from the Advisor Book.
Goal: every complaint in the shop scored within 48 hours, every recovered customer called within 7 days.

Chapter 13

Champion Development, Calibration & Train-the-Trainer

A coach who does everything forever is a bottleneck. Build successors, keep scoring honest, and make the system survive you.

Champion Development — Teach It to Own It

Champions run drills, lead huddles, and take mastery checks. Handing rituals to Champions is not delegation for convenience — it's how culture becomes self-sustaining and how the Champion earns their level. The rules:

  • Assign one ritual at a time, permanently. "You own Tuesday's Take-Five from now on" beats "help me with drills."
  • Coach the Champion, not the room. When a Champion runs the drill, your job is to coach the Champion's coaching — praise in public, correct in private, SBI format.
  • Shout out Champion moments publicly. Which Champion moment deserved a public shout-out this week? If you can't answer, you're under-watching your best people.

Psychological Safety — The Coaching Substrate

Advisors will not surface mistakes, ask for help, or try new behaviors if they fear being shamed. Your most invisible job is maintaining the conditions under which people are willing to be imperfect.

"What were you trying to do?" before "here's what I saw.""Why did you…" — puts the advisor on defense.

Calibration — Making "Mastery" Mean the Same Thing Across Coaches

Two coaches grading the same call 4/5 vs. 2/5 means there is no shared standard — which means promotions feel arbitrary, which means trust in the system erodes. Run calibration monthly.

🟢 Drill · The Monthly Calibration Session Setup: pick three recorded calls representing a range — one great, one middle, one weak. All coaches score independently using the Ride-Along Scorecard.
Reveal: scores read aloud. Where the spread is more than 1 point, discuss the specific moment that drove the variance. Agree on a shared rubric.
Goal: within three months, inter-coach variance below 0.5 points. That's when promotions become trustworthy.

Train-the-Trainer — The Backup-Coach Ladder

The single biggest risk to the system at any shop is losing the coach — promotion, operations, departure. Begin developing a second coach inside rollout Phase 3. Inside the first year, every shop should have a backup coach who can run the full cadence solo for two weeks.
